Phase 4: Consolidation of Base Areas

Early 1948 — the Communists turn scattered “liberated areas” into functioning regions they can govern and supply, especially across North China and the central plains.

From Guerrilla Bases to Real Governments

After the successful defenses and counterattacks of 1947, the CCP focused on turning military control into lasting power. In many rural counties they created local governments, people’s militias, and simple courts; carried out deeper land reform; and organized taxes and grain collection to feed the army. Ordinary villagers now experienced Communist rule in daily life, not just as passing troops.

Weakening the Nationalists

As Communist base areas expanded, isolated KMT garrisons in the north and central plains lost the countryside around them. Inflation, food shortages, and harsh tax collection in Nationalist‑held zones pushed more people toward the CCP, while peasant militias and PLA units cut roads and railways. The KMT still held many major cities, but it was steadily losing the villages that supplied food, recruits, and intelligence.

Revolution, Reaction, and Reform

In this phase, the Communist revolution became a full alternative state—complete with its own schools, courts, and local leaders. The KMT reacted with blockades and punitive campaigns, but corruption and poor logistics undercut those efforts. Inside the bases, ongoing land reform, literacy drives, and basic health work convinced many peasants that CCP rule was more just than the old landlord‑KMT system. These consolidated areas supplied the men and resources that would power the decisive campaigns of late 1948.
