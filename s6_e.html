Phase 6: The Yangtze Crossing

April - May 1949 — The PLA breaches the Yangtze River, historically the dividing line between north and south China, and captures the Nationalist capital.

Overview: Breaking the Last Great Barrier

The Yangtze Crossing (April - May 1949)

For centuries, the Yangtze River symbolized the division between north and south China. In spring 1949, the People's Liberation Army crossed this historic barrier, effectively ending Nationalist control of mainland China. The fall of Nanjing, the Nationalist capital, and the subsequent capture of Shanghai and other major southern cities demonstrated that Communist victory was now complete. The Nationalists, facing defeat, retreated to Taiwan.

  • Strategic Importance: The Yangtze crossing eliminated the Nationalists' last natural defensive line and opened southern China to Communist forces.
  • Military Success: PLA forces deployed thousands of boats and amphibious units to cross the heavily fortified river.
  • Rapid Collapse: Nationalist resistance crumbled quickly; garrisons either surrendered, fled, or switched sides.
  • Symbolic Victory: Crossing the Yangtze meant the Communists now controlled all major population centers in mainland China.

The Crossing Operations

April 1949: The Assault Begins

On April 20-21, 1949, PLA forces launched their assault across the Yangtze at multiple points, overwhelming weak Nationalist defenses.

  • Initial Crossings: The 2nd and 3rd Field Armies crossed the Yangtze near Anqing and Kopang, encountering minimal resistance.
  • Rapid Advance: Within days, PLA forces had secured beachheads on the southern bank and advanced inland.
  • Nationalist Retreat: KMT forces were either destroyed, captured, or retreated southward in disarray.
  • Number of Troops: Over 150,000 PLA troops crossed initially, supported by hastily constructed boats and captured vessels.

Fall of Nanjing (April 23, 1949)

Nanjing (then romanized as Nanking), the Nationalist capital and heart of their southern resistance, fell to PLA forces with surprisingly little resistance.

  • The Evacuation: Chiang Kai-shek and most senior Nationalist leaders had already fled to Canton (Guangzhou) and then to Taiwan.
  • The Fall: The city was encircled and fell within days. Nationalist defenders either surrendered or deserted.
  • Symbolic Loss: The fall of the capital symbolized the definitive end of Nationalist authority on the mainland.
  • Casualties: Far fewer than in previous campaigns; most defending units had already been destroyed or demoralized.

Shanghai Falls (May 27, 1949)

Shanghai, China's largest city and economic center, surrendered to the PLA after brief fighting, completing the conquest of China's major urban centers.

  • Siege and Battle: The PLA encircled Shanghai and attacked from multiple directions; Nationalist forces put up limited resistance before withdrawing.
  • Urban Warfare: Unlike rural areas, urban combat was brief; the city's defending forces were weak and morale was shattered.
  • Economic Significance: Control of Shanghai gave the Communists access to China's financial and commercial hub.
  • Strategic Result: The Nationalists now held only scattered territory in the southwest and Taiwan.

Why Nationalist Resistance Collapsed

The Final Breakdown

  • Destroyed Army: The elite forces destroyed in the Three Great Campaigns of 1948-1949 could not be replaced.
  • Lost Faith: Soldiers and civilians no longer believed the Nationalists could win; surrender or defection seemed inevitable.
  • Economic Collapse: Hyperinflation had destroyed the currency; soldiers were unpaid and unfed.
  • Political Paralysis: With Chiang Kai-shek resigned (though still influential), the government lacked coherent leadership.
  • PLA Dominance: The Communist forces had become vastly superior in numbers, discipline, and popular support.

Aftermath and Significance

The Nationalist Retreat to Taiwan

By late May 1949, the Nationalist government and military remnants retreated to Taiwan, a large island off the Chinese coast. What had been envisioned as a temporary withdrawal became permanent; the Nationalists established the "Republic of China" on Taiwan, while Mao prepared to proclaim the People's Republic on the mainland.

Legacy: The Yangtze crossing marked the effective end of the Chinese Civil War. Though scattered Nationalist forces remained on isolated islands and in remote southwestern regions, organized Nationalist government was restricted to Taiwan. The stage was now set for Mao Zedong to proclaim the People's Republic of China on October 1, 1949.

Revolution, Reaction, and Reform

Revolution

The Yangtze crossing turned the Communist revolution into full control of China’s political and economic heartland. By breaking the last great natural barrier and capturing Nanjing and Shanghai, the PLA showed that Nationalist power on the mainland was finished.

Reaction

The Nationalists reacted by abandoning one capital after another, retreating south and then offshore to Taiwan. Instead of organizing a strong final defense, many units collapsed, surrendered, or fled, and Chiang Kai-shek prepared for a long-term government-in-exile.

Reform

As they took over the lower Yangtze and coastal cities, the Communists began shifting from wartime mobilization to governing—promising to restore order, pay wages, control inflation, and punish corruption, while planning broader land and social reforms that would follow nationwide victory.
